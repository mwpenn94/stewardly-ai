Landscape pass — signals: the work has not been challenged, alternatives have not been explored, or obvious gaps are visible. Action: Exhaustively identify every optimization, improvement, correction, enhancement, and missed opportunity, including those from adjacent or non-obvious domains. Immediately conduct any necessary research to confirm or discover superior methods and integrate findings without delay. Surface only what is missing or suboptimal — do not restate strengths or redundancies. Implement all identified improvements in the output — do not merely list them. If improvements conflict with each other, state the conflict, choose the higher-leverage option, and note what was traded away.

Depth pass — signals: broad coverage exists but specific areas remain shallow, assumptions are untested, or internal contradictions exist. Action: Attack the weakest areas. Stress-test every assumption against real-world conditions, edge cases, and scale. Replace every instance of "good enough" with "best possible within current constraints." Identify where conventional wisdom is wrong for this specific context. Resolve tensions between prior improvements. Where the work involves competing objectives — such as speed versus quality, simplicity versus completeness, or cost versus performance — explicitly state the tradeoff, clarify which side you are optimizing for and why, and provide alternative configurations if the user's priority differs.

Adversarial pass — signals: the work appears solid on the surface with no obvious gaps remaining. Action: Assume everything contains hidden failure modes, blind spots, premature optimization, and obsolescence risk. Identify what breaks under edge cases, scale, time pressure, resource constraints, or hostile conditions. Detect what looks right but degrades over time. Anticipate critiques from world-class experts. Identify silent failures — where the system appears to work but produces wrong results without visible error signals. Scrutinize the interplay of all components for loopholes, ambiguities, or unintended consequences. Fix every issue found. If two fixes conflict, prioritize the one that prevents silent failure over the one that improves visible behavior — silent failures are strictly worse than visible ones. Specific failure classes to check: Goodhart's Law (optimizing for the metric instead of the goal), silent degradation (components that fail gracefully into uselessness), scope creep during execution, regression from prior improvements, and single points of dependency.

Future-State and Synthesis pass — signals: the work has survived adversarial scrutiny and current-state optimization is near exhaustion. Action: Project forward 12, 24, and 36 months. Identify emerging technologies, regulatory shifts, market dynamics, or paradigm changes that would alter the optimal approach. Integrate all improvements into a cohesive, future-proof architecture. Eliminate redundancy, resolve conflicts, and ensure every element earns its place. The final output of this pass must stand alone as a complete, production-ready deliverable — not a layer of notes on top of prior passes.

Fundamental Redesign — signals: the work's core structure, premise, or framing is flawed in a way that incremental optimization cannot fix. This overrides all other pass types. Action: Before rebuilding, explicitly state what is broken, why incremental improvement is insufficient to fix it, and what you are preserving from the existing work. Then rebuild from the ground up, carrying forward only what survives scrutiny.
